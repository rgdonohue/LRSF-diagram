Retail, Restaurant, and Packaged Goods

Equitable Response &
Recovery

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Small, independent operators, local restaurants, food entrepreneurs, and independent grocers are uniquely impacted by disruptions like COVID-19 and climate crises because of limited access to cash reserves to weather the storm.
  • Owner operators and employees of these businesses are disproportionately impacted by the front-line risk of continuing essential food access services.

Resilience in Action:

  • The Nourish Our Neighbors program run by Hope & Main Food Incubator in Rhode Island was launched in response to increased levels of food insecurity brought on by the COVID-19 pandemic. Through a buy one/give one model, the program provided meals to food insecure families and vulnerable individuals.
  • Many new-immigrant and historically marginalized entrepreneurs are utilizing shared kitchens to start food businesses. The Network for Incubator and Commissary Kitchens helps food entrepreneurs exchange best practices, solve problems, and connect with other shared-use kitchen users.

 

Securing
Supply Chains

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • With critical access to power (especially for cold storage), gas, and transportation of goods, locally embedded operations can serve as points of emergency service provision and maintain continuity of markets. This, in turn, has significant consequences for the safety and quality of food and community food access.
  • Without access to affordable and scale-appropriate online sales platforms, independent food retailers and value-added producers struggle to pivot to online and alternative sales platforms.

Resilience in Action:

  • Community Food Coops are Thriving During the Pandemic, National Coop Grocers: Independent grocers and coops that had established relationships with producers and suppliers were better able to keep shelves full in times of supply chain shortages.
  • New Orleans' Get Lit Stay Lit initiative is one example of how by using a local and regional food system-level response, restaurants coordinated with response efforts in their communities to use existing cold storage infrastructure during climate-related food systems disruptions.

 

Maintaining
Markets

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • When the COVID-19 pandemic hit, restaurants faced dire financial and operational circumstances as businesses closed because of lockdowns.
  • Restaurant closures significantly disrupted the relationships between local producers and restaurants. Overnight, farmers lost valuable markets as restaurants struggled to maintain operations.
  • Value-added food entrepreneurs who are commonly reliant on direct-to-consumer markets or sales to independent grocers and restaurants are forced to pivot to entirely new sales models to reach consumers amid disruption.

Resilience in Action:

  • Restaurant Reboot Relief Program Innovation Brief : The Lee Initiative provided stipends to local farms in 20 regions across the U.S. to ensure continued food production for restaurants and to maintain producer-restaurant relationships. This allowed restaurants to reduce ingredient costs while still acquiring local products, providing high-quality food to patrons, retaining restaurant workers, and maintaining markets for and relationships with farmers.

Institutions and Wholesale

Equitable Response &
Recovery

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • LRFS wholesale producers can be left out of conversations on re-configuring supply chains and institutional dining, resulting in approaches that don’t align with producers’ capacity.
  • Abrupt shutdowns of schools disrupt classroom learning and limit access to school meals, a vital food resource for families and source of nutrition for many children.
  • A sustained and intentional commitment to equity and inclusion in response and recovery efforts can:
    • direct resources to support continuation of essential markets
    • maintain and advance nutrition security
    • lead to the long-term growth of market opportunities.

Resilience in Action:

  • From Food Hubs to CSAs: Food hubs and institutional dining operations leverage established and equitably developed relationships and networks to ensure they are uniquely positioned to aggregate and connect local food producers to unmet needs in hunger relief programming.
  • Farm Fresh Food Network, developed by FreshApproach, provides long-term opportunities and market channels for small and beginning farmers, BIPOC and woman-owned farms within the hunger relief ecosystem of the San Francisco Bay area. Using a hub-and-spoke model, they delivered local farm boxes under the Farmers to Families Food Box Program.

Securing
Supply Chains

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Institutional and wholesale markets experience both supply chain shortages and dramatic financial loss in times of crisis.
  • Consumers reliant on cafeterias, restaurants, and other wholesale outlets are significantly impacted by reduced food access points when these outlets close.
  • Food producers who have invested significant time and money into growing mid-tier markets may be left with abundant product and no clear way to get it into the marketplace because of inflexibility and lack of diversity within consolidated food supply chains.

Resilience in Action:

  • Access Point Innovation Brief: Schools can become central distribution points for various types of food share programs ranging from Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) shares, local food boxes, or grocery bags by partnering with philanthropic organizations, city governments, and non-profit community organizations.
  • From Food Hubs to CSAs Innovation Brief: Because of their investments in mid-sized infrastructure and value chains, food hubs and wholesale value chains provide essential built and soft infrastructure for farmers and communities in times of disruption. This infrastructure helps to create and grow market channels for farmers and establishes avenues to get food to people in need.
  • Food Hub Infrastructure in Hawaii: On islands and in other geographically remote communities, mid-sized value chain infrastructure can adapt to the unique geography and context of the communities.

Maintaining
Markets

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • During public health crises, dining programs at health care facilities are impacted by reduced visitation combined with unexpected PPE costs, drastic reduction in outpatient procedures, slashed budgets, and overburdened staff. This can lead to temporary or permanent discontinuation of local procurement initiatives.
  • When business as usual is no longer an option, LRFS wholesale markets find ways to shift operations from institutions and restaurants to direct service models, often in a way focused on hunger relief.
  • The strength of many food hubs’ community partnerships allows them to transition quickly when other parts of the food supply chain slow or shut down.

Resilience in Action:

  • Healthcare CSAs and Local Retail Outlets Innovation Brief: Healthcare facilities implemented and expanded local food initiatives during the pandemic to effectively feed and care for constituents while also investing in the local food economy. By leveraging existing partnerships and value chains, healthcare organizations moved quickly to serve their stakeholders through onsite retail (e.g., grocery) locations and expanded community supported agriculture (CSA) or produce subscription programs.
  • The Good Acre's Local Emergency Assistance Farmer Fund is an example of how food hubs co-created programs specifically and explicitly designed to create sustainable market channels with/by/for BIPOC producers. Using elements of a Community Supported Agriculture model, local food hubs collaborated with partners to match the needs of producers and consumers amid the pandemic-induced market shift. Pre-established partnerships helped hubs understand and navigate new supply and demand landscapes when their traditional wholesale market models evaporated.

Government and Policy

Equitable Response &
Recovery

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Disruptions like COVID-19 and climate emergencies result in extreme and diverse LRFS impacts across stakeholder groups, including overwhelming food banks with the heightened need for their services, leaving vulnerable populations without reliable transportation to access food, and farmers being left with product in the field because they lack appropriate market channels.
  • Agencies at all levels of government (Municipal, State, Tribal, Federal) are called on to support and coordinate local efforts while also coordinating with federal emergency response agencies and initiatives to shift funding and priorities to meet the needs of those most impacted. Agencies can shift and prioritize funding to serve communities and individuals most impacted by the disruption.

Resilience in Action:

  • Passed in the 2021 state legislative session, CT Grown for CT Kids works to remedy the gap between school lunch programs and nearby farms. This statewide grant program, run through the Connecticut Department of Agriculture, funded efforts that connect students to local agriculture through food education, local sourcing, and community partnerships. Read more about CT Grown for CT Kids here.

Securing
Supply Chains

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Impacts to LRFS infrastructure and supply chains can come from damage to on-farm infrastructure, loss of power supply to essential cold-chain equipment, or disruption to social infrastructure like community aid networks.
  • Farmers and food businesses have a difficult time accessing and navigating federal relief funds and cost share programs as dissemination of information about how to access different sources of funding for damaged and/or destroyed on-farm and consumer-focused infrastructure like fences, barns, and farm stands is lacking.

Resilience in Action:

  • The Pandemic Response and Safety (PRS) Grant Program provided approximately $650 million in grants to food processors, distributors, farmers markets, and producers to respond to the coronavirus, including worker protection against COVID-19.

Maintaining
Markets

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Policy and regulation determine what types of operations continue, what activities are shut down, and guide flows of resources through impacted communities. With community-informed approaches, government-lead response efforts can integrate and attend to the unique needs of LRFS farm and food enterprises, thereby providing critical market opportunities for local producers, food access to vulnerable communities, and new infrastructure pathways.
  • Investments to bolster and strengthen market pathways may ensure more robust and diverse market opportunities that can be sustained following crises.

Resilience in Action:

  • In Iowa, the Local Produce and Protein Program was instrumental in strengthening farm to school initiatives. Funded by CARES Act funding, this program incentivized school activities across the supply chain to create local food connections and teach kids about their food. The program's core goals were to (1) connect k-12 schools with local producers; (2) provide grant funding to help schools purchase from those local suppliers; and (3) provide grant funding for producers to purchase the supplies needed to produce and distribute in the school setting.

Non-Commodity Goods

Equitable Response &
Recovery

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Wholesale accounts with restaurants and institutions are often major portions of sales for artisan bakeries and specialty meat producers. Disruptions in restaurants and institutions in turn negatively impact market outlets for the non-commodity goods sector.
  • Limited infrastructure for retail packaging and underdeveloped retail distribution of grain-based products and meats can lead to significant bottlenecks and consumer shortages.

Resilience in Action:

  • Neighbor Loaves Innovation Brief: The Artisan Grain Collaborative, a collective of Mid-Western bakers, chefs, millers, and farmers, saw an opportunity to support those struggling with the loss of wholesale accounts while helping community feeding organizations meet increased demand. Through Neighbor Loaves, bakeries distributed bread to community feeding organizations while also generating a key source of revenue for bakeries hit by COVID-19 market disruptions while building markets for local grains.

Securing
Supply Chains

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Disruptions to consolidated and vertically integrated meat processing supply chains result in an overwhelming demand for products and services from mid-tier and small-scale livestock processing systems. This in turn causes unprecedented delays in service.

Resilience in Action:

  • State Investments and Innovations in Meat Processing: Increasing small and mid-tier processing and distribution capacity via new facilities, equipment, and training staff requires long term strategy and investment. The development of local and regional markets for non-commodity and niche products requires an engaged consumer base and regional value chains to sustain demand during and beyond times of disruption.

Maintaining
Markets

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Abrupt pivots from wholesale to direct to consumer and retail sales pose challenges for production and business models designed to move product at maximum volume.
  • The seafood sector is especially enmeshed with restaurant sales. Challenges integrating into emergency food operations, the loss of restaurant sales, lower prices for seafood, and disruptions of markets caused revenue to decline in the fishing industry.

Resilience in Action:

  • The Local Catch Network Impact Assessment: Within the seafood sector, fishermen had to completely rethink how they conducted business due to a loss of markets. With restaurants closed, many fishermen started selling their seafood directly to customers off-the-boat and on-the-dock and there was a boom in online sales for direct seafood marketing. The Local Catch Network connected fishermen involved in local and direct seafood marketing with local and regional consumers.

Direct to Consumer

Equitable Response &
Recovery

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Diversified direct to consumer operations with undesignated produce and/or livestock growing in the fields face distinct challenges in pivoting their business to ensure their product finds a market when their normal markets are shut down or unreachable.
  • Geographically remote (e.g., rural, island, and other remote communities) LRFS producers are especially vulnerable to market disruption due to limited access to alternative markets.

Resilience in Action:

  • Thinking Inside the Box Innovation Brief: Farmers markets and CSAs pivoted to food box programs and curbside and drive-thru models to continue market access while also continuing SNAP/EBT payment and nutrition incentive program access.
  • What is a Sliding Scale You Ask?, developed by Underground Alchemy: This resource explains how sliding-scale CSAs are tools for building economic justice while simultaneously ensuring fair compensation to producers.

Securing
Supply Chains

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Long standing and well-known market infrastructure and locations (e.g., farmers market pavilions, roadside stands) are displaced or shut down due to evacuations and/or public health restrictions.

Resilience in Action:

  • Alternative Farmers Market Models Innovation Brief: By leveraging social media as a key communication platform and partnering with municipal and NGO partners, farmers markets and other direct to consumer outlets rapidly established alternative market locations and drive-through models.

Maintaining
Markets

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Shelter in place protocols coupled with supply chain disruptions for conventional food sources spurred a boom in demand for mail order, door to door delivery, and other delivery-based direct market outlets.
  • Uncertainty regarding the viability or longevity of novel market channels can leave farm and food businesses in limbo regarding future business plans.

Resilience in Action:

  • Partner Shares from the FairShare CSA Coalition used CARES Act funding to expand existing food assistance programs and more than doubled their assistance for low-cost CSA boxes for families across the Midwest.
  • Farmer to Farmer Exchange, ECommerce Platforms, CSA Innovation Network: Provides ratings and detailed insight from producers on the quality, efficacy, and ease of use of on-line sales platforms. The report is intended to support producers in decision making as they had to quickly adapt to online sale systems, contactless payments, and contactless deliveries.

Community, Faith-Based, and NGOs

Equitable Response &
Recovery

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Disruptions and natural disaster often throw people into food insecurity for the first time. These individuals and families are unfamiliar with how to access and navigate food security resources.
  • Food banks and other food access programs must adapt and accommodate the influx of new people using their programs and conduct additional outreach to help reach everyone in need.

Resilience in Action:

  • Emergency Food, Charity, and the Local Food System Webinar Highlights: Amid crisis, community organizations established community feeding programs to help meet immediate need. Two examples are Spirit Lake Tribe's Food Distribution Program that operated a grocery store style food bank and placed a large emphasis on culturally appropriate foods and Nourish Lexington which pivoted to prepare and distribute fresh, nutritious meals to anyone in need as emergency feeding became a vital aspect of pandemic response.

Securing
Supply Chains

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Supply chain shortages and sharp upticks in demand from newly food insecure community members can overwhelm emergency food systems.
  • The COVID-19 pandemic created health and safety concerns around standard food distribution and delivery models.
  • Evacuations resulting from natural disasters create mobile populations with limited access to kitchen facilities, cold storage, or familiar places for food procurement.

Resilience in Action:

  • Building a Decentralized Food Supply Chain in Puerto Rico: Communities leveraged LRFS' inherent strengths of diversified and dispersed food production and distribution systems to establish partnerships between community-based organizations and emergency/charitable feeding operations.

Maintaining
Markets

Impacts and Response in LRFS:

  • Community and faith-based food systems initiatives are uniquely positioned to identify, understand, and respond to the needs of historically underserved and vulnerable communities.
  • Drawing on longstanding traditions of mutual-aid and cooperation, these groups can mobilize quickly to connect their communities with emergency feeding programs.
  • Cooperative economic models deployed by community groups support underserved food producers in times of disruption and beyond.

Resilience in Action:

  • The Black Church Food Security Network's Soil to Sanctuary Market program is an example of how communities with existing connections to local farms and food producers used those relationships to bring locally produced foods into the overtaxed emergency food system. This ensured that healthy and culturally relevant foods were available to neighbors in need.
